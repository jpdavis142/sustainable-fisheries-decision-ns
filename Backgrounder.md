

# Backgrounder

Marine ecosystems are essential to global biodiversity, food security, and economic activity, particularly in coastal regions such as Nova Scotia. The fisheries sector plays a significant role in the provincial economy by supporting employment, exports, and the livelihoods of coastal communities. However, growing pressures on marine environments—including overfishing, climate change, and habitat degradation—have made sustainable management increasingly urgent. These pressures are directly tied to the United Nations Sustainable Development Goal 14 (Life Below Water), which emphasizes the conservation and sustainable use of ocean resources.

This decision matters because it involves balancing environmental sustainability with economic stability. On one hand, declining fish populations threaten long-term ecosystem health and the future viability of fisheries. If current harvesting practices continue unchecked, fish stocks may collapse, leading to severe ecological and economic consequences, as seen in historical examples such as the Atlantic cod fishery collapse. On the other hand, implementing stricter catch limits could reduce short-term income for fishers and disrupt communities that rely heavily on fishing as a primary source of revenue. This creates a significant policy challenge where both action and inaction carry meaningful risks.

The difficulty of this decision lies in the tradeoffs and system dynamics involved. Increasing fishing intensity can boost short-term catch volumes and income, but it simultaneously reduces fish populations, which undermines future productivity. Conversely, stricter regulations can allow fish stocks to recover over time, improving long-term sustainability but at the cost of immediate economic hardship. These competing outcomes are reinforced through feedback loops, where economic incentives drive behavior that impacts ecological conditions, which in turn influence future economic outcomes. This complexity makes the decision non-obvious and highly dependent on how short-term versus long-term priorities are weighted.

Historically, fisheries management has relied on tools such as quotas, seasonal restrictions, and conservation policies. While these approaches have had varying levels of success, challenges remain in enforcement, data reliability, and adapting to changing environmental conditions. Recent shifts toward ecosystem-based management and sustainability-focused policies reflect growing recognition that long-term resource health must be prioritized. However, achieving this balance remains difficult due to conflicting stakeholder interests and uncertainty in predicting ecological responses.

This project will evaluate whether stricter catch limits represent a viable strategy for achieving long-term sustainability without causing unacceptable economic disruption. By analyzing system relationships and available data, the goal is to better understand how fisheries policy decisions can align environmental conservation with economic resilience.

## Key Stakeholders

Several stakeholders are directly affected by fisheries policy decisions, each with distinct priorities and interests:

**Commercial Fishers**  
Fishers are primarily concerned with income stability and access to fishing resources. Stricter catch limits may reduce their short-term earnings and create financial uncertainty.

**Coastal Communities**  
Many communities in Nova Scotia rely heavily on the fishing industry for employment and economic activity. Changes in policy can have broader social and economic impacts beyond individual fishers.

**Government and Policy Makers**  
Regulators are responsible for balancing economic performance with environmental sustainability. They must also meet national and international commitments, including SDG 14.

**Environmental Organizations**  
These groups prioritize the protection of marine ecosystems and advocate for sustainable fishing practices to prevent long-term ecological damage.

**Seafood Industry (Processors and Exporters)**  
Businesses depend on a stable and predictable supply of fish. Overfishing threatens long-term supply chains, while stricter regulations may reduce short-term availability.

## Datasets

The following datasets will be used to support analysis and decision-making:

**1. Protection of the Rights of Small-Scale Fisheries Dataset**  
This dataset provides information on policies and protections for small-scale fisheries across different regions. It will be used to analyze how governance and regulatory frameworks influence sustainability and economic outcomes.  
*Source: protection-of-the-rights-of-small-scale-fisheries.csv*

**2. Capture Fisheries and Aquaculture Production Dataset (SDG 14)**  
This dataset contains data on capture fisheries and aquaculture production levels. It will be used to examine trends in fish production and assess how fishing intensity relates to sustainability indicators.  
*Source: goal14.capture_aquaculture.csv*
